How do I choose the right amount?

The main goal of your emergency fund is to prepare for things you can’t predict. But thinking ahead to potential scenarios can help ensure you’re prepared. Consider the following when deciding whether to target the higher end or lower end of your recommended savings range.

Think of upcoming expenses you might have soon
  • A replacement hot water heater
  • New brake pads for your car
  • An unplanned family visit
Consider any potential instability in either partner’s job
  • If there has been or may be any layoffs
  • If your current company offers (or does not offer) a severance package
Understand how market volatility might impact your available funds
When the market is down, your investment balance might be lower than you'd like when you need funds.

Frequently Asked Questions
How did we get this target savings range?
We use artificial intelligence and a custom algorithm to calculate your individual target. The calculation is based on personal factors like your income and expense data from your Investor Profile, and the answers to questions about your job and education. This information is combined with historical data on job loss and recovery to help to estimate savings needed to cover you for a potential loss of income.
What are emergency savings options beyond savings accounts?
Accounts that can provide both security and returns:
  • Money market – investments that are like cash, with higher returns.
  • General investment accounts – including ETFs, stocks, this money has been taxed, and returns are taxed, but it can grow at a higher rate than money markets or high-yield savings accounts.
  • Roth IRAs – Contributions to a Roth IRA can be withdrawn without tax or penalty and, if the emergency you’re saving for never arises, you’ll enjoy tax-free growth and progress toward your long-term retirement goals.
  • HSAs – Contributions to your HSA are yours to use, over your lifetime. They're only eligible to use for medical expenses in most cases, but can come in handy for an unexpected or large medical bill.
Should I be concerned about my emergency funds fluctuating in value?
Over time, no. But if you need to use funds when markets are down, it can be good to have cash reserves as a buffer. Look at how the investments you earmark for your emergency fund are allocated. If the assets are heavily invested in equities or other investments that fluctuate in value, you may want to consider:
  • Increasing your total target savings amount to be at the higher end of the range
  • Adjusting your total target savings to include a greater percentage of cash
